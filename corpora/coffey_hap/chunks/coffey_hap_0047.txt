And once he was securely in the Army, where he no longer had to cope with his father's pressure, he began to supply his own, creating it for himself by his refusal to accept conventional military wisdom. He was headstrong, often tactless, scornful of slow, conservative Army methods, critical of superiors, single-mindedly relentless in pushing his own beliets, and impatient with those who failed to share his vision. Yet despite all this, he rose through the officer ranks as the years passed until, in September 1938, he became chief of the Air Corps. To the surprise of many military men, including Arnold himself, the Army establishment proved itself sufficiently flexible and tolerant of his independence to advance him to the top.
