He had just returned from Alaska, where he had supervised the construction of a telegraph network, but that was not the subject of his visit to Arnold. From Alaska he had made a short trip to Japan, where he had studied the Japanese army, to the extent that he was permitted, and had learned that the Japanese air force already had ten planes, which was more than the United States could boast. His experiences in Japan, plus his expectations about coming developments in aircraft capability, had prompted him to embark on research for a paper he intended to submit to the General Staff about the military future of aviation  Since his visitor had never flown an airplane, Arnold may have been astonished by his presumption, but he was also impressed by his knowledge. The man wasn’t really interested in carrying on a conversa- tion.
