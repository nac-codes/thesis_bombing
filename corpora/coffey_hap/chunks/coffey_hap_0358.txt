When Eaker met Colonel Arnold, he decided he was looking at the most handsome Army officer he had ever seen, with the possible exception of General Pershing. “He was six feet tall, erect, wore his uniform with pride and grace,” Eaker later recalled. “His instant trademark was a quick, engaging smile, but he possessed a reserve and dignity of bearing which did not encourage familiarity.”'  The war years in Washington, although frustrating to Arnold, had accustomed him to authority, and had given him a sense of confidence in his own ideas. So many things he favored had worked and so many things he disapproved of had failed, especially in the field of aircraft development, that he saw no reason to be timid about his ideas.
