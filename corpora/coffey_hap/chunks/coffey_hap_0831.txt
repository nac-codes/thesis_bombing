Beaverbrook listened to Arnold talk about the planes and materiel and men he considered necessary to win the war, then called Churchill and suggested the prime minister interview this American airman. Beaverbrook also immediately granted Arnold's request for two Spitfires, two Hurricanes (both fighters), two Wellingtons (medium bombers), and two night fighters for American experts to study. And he cordially invited Arnold to see “everything the British had.” But most important from an air strategist's vie wpoint, he told Arnold that the Germans had reduced British aircraft production by as much as one-third in some months by bombing their factories. And the Germans never used more than five hundred planes. Arnold, who envisioned the use of eight hundred or one thousand planes per raid became increasingly confident that American air power, when fully realized, would have the capability of winning this war for the Allies.
