After the opening moves, during which some of the belligerents also engaged in large-scale maneuver warfare, World War II also developed into a struggle of attrition. This was not because there was prolonged stalemate at the front—there was not—but because the oppos- ing sides were so large, or else ruled such vast stretches of land and sea, that even the largest operational movements, such as the Battle of Stalingrad and its successors, did not suffice to bring about a decision. Time after time, the front stabilized and the attacker, who was usually one of the Allies, had to start all over again. Inevitably, attrition meant a long war, and inevitably, a long war meant that the factories were called upon to play a critical role. In 1939 the power with the greatest industrial potential was the United States. If we put that potential at 3, then that of the runner-up, Germany, stood at 1.2.
