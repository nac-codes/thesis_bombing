In summer 1941, Major Haywood Hansell visited the Air Ministry, where Air Vice Marshal Inglis gave him nearly a ton of documents, mostly target folders, to assist the USAAF air staff with targeting. Hansell requisitioned an American bomber to haul this treasure trove back to Washington, D.C., where the documents augmented a small but rap- idly growing collection of target materials in ACAS A-2.Â©2  Despite these important developments, the Allies entered World War II without anything approaching a mature air intelligence organization. However, the British had come much closer by Septem- ber 1939 than the Americans had by December 1941. The reasons for this had to do with a greater British appreciation for the importance of air and economic intelligence agencies, working in close cooperation, and fed with a steady stream of aerial photographs and, increasingly once the war began, Ultra decrypts. The Americans, on the other hand, focused on bombing doctrine, heavy bombers, and advanced bombsights. A de facto operational capability had emerged by the time they entered the war.
