Henry Alexander was relieved that the “judge” who would listen to “both sides of the case” that he and the other Directors felt was lacking in their earlier discussions seemed “to have been found” in General Spaatz.6 9  The meeting with the General went very well for the Survey and their alternate bombardment plan for the defeat of Japan. D’Olier spoke with Anderson on the phone shortly after the meeting on the 18th and crowed: “everything has wound up very, very satisfactorily.” D’Olier claimed that they had convinced General Spaatz (and AAF Deputy Chief of Staff, General Eaker, who was also at the meeting) that transportation was “absolutely the prime target.” The AAF was “going at it just as soon” as possible, boasted the Chairman.7 0  General Eaker noted that Spaatz “was inclined to concur with the D’Olier Committee’s recommendation...[the] disruption of Japanese transportation is of such significant importance that an overwhelming attack on transportation may well have a direct and early effect on the other priorities.”7 1 Spaatz and other airmen thought that American air power probably could end the war against Japan, thereby eliminating the need for an “other” priority— the land invasion.7 2 As the newly assigned commander of the U.S. Army Strategic Air Forces in the Pacific, Spaatz, in accordance with a directive from General Arnold, intended to implement the plan.7 3  As a devoted advocate of precision bombing doctrine, Spaatz certainly must have 6 9  Nitze to Alexander, 14 July 1945, Box 41, file 383.4, RG 243, NA. 7 0  “Record of Telephone Conversation, 18 July 1945,” Box 22, file 311.3, RG 243, NA. 7 1  Personal from Eaker to Arnold, 18 July 1945, Box 2, file 001, RG 243, NA. 7 2 Mets, Master o f Airpower,  303; also see, Wedemeyerto Lincoln, 10 July 1945, Box 5, George A.
