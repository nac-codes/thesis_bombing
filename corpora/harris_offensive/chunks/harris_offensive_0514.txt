All this did not, of course, benefit Bomber Command itself or cut down our losses, because whatever happened the night fighter force was unlikely to incur heavy casualties and did not have to fight any other fighters except an occasional Mosquito in the bomber stream. But the Allied forces as a whole were well prepared for the invasion of Europe now that strategic bombing had put the enemy so completely on the defensive and in addition done very considerable harm to the aircraft industry. The capture of the Channel ports. The flying bombs. A night-fighter lands in England.  N THE face of it, the invasion of Europe seemed to present  the Western allies with an insoluble problem. The Germans  evidently thought so, and took the risk of putting their optimistic appreciation of the situation before the German people. There was first the “ Atlantic Wall,” a ring of strong fortifications along all the accessible stretches of the coast, with very long range, very heavy, radar-sighted guns which could sink any warships before these could shoot back, and, of course, could smash any convoy to pieces. The beach obstacles and other con- structional works were equally strong.
