First, while the Japanese were still on the offensive, the seemingly suicidal nature of their resistance hardly appeared; even into 1943 they sometimes retreated rather than fight to the death. Furthermore, evidence of Japanese mistreatment of prisoners of war accumulated slowly and was even more slowly released to the American public. The passions and hatreds of war mounted gradually in response to months of sustained defeat and frustration and to the manner in which dominant institutions interpreted these phenomena.  Popular expectations for the strategic course of the war remained similarly diffuse. Many Americans, moved by anglophobia or by panic over events in the Far East, challenged the administra- tion’s Europe-first priority and later its quest for unconditional surrender. Others suspected that the American stake in the Far East was imperial rather than moral or strategic. To some observers, the popular mood seemed variously listless and quixotic, given over first to “a kind of puzzled boredom about the war in general.” then to spurts of excessive optimism at the first signs of success. “Daily one hears talk of the war being over in six months,” commented a British observer after November’s landings in North Africa.
