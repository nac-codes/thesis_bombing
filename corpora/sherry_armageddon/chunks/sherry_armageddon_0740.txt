Perhaps the acceptance of strategic air war meant that Americans had suspended moral debate, accepted the bomber on utilitarian grounds, and revealed again their supposed “tendency to refer to war in absolute terms.”>5 But while the religious and pacifist press sometimes displayed that tendency, its stance and role in wartime debate also revealed more complex reasons for the accept- ance of air war.  Implicitly or explicitly, it is true, the religious press often confessed its inability to advocate restraints on the conduct of air war. Reacting to the Doolittle raid, the Christian Century could only express regret about the intensification of air war and “commit to the mercy of God those upon whom the bombs may fall.” One source of restraint, arising out of prewar law and theology, lay in the test of utility, the principle that “greater violence must not be used than is necessary to achieve the purpose in view.” But there seemed no way “to draw a line between discriminate and indiscriminate bombing.” Indeed, it seemed “idle to try to put a check upon the way in which weapons are used.
