LeMay had little time or interest to explore the strategy or politics of air power. Before the war his rank had been too junior to place him in the service schools and the planning offices where these matters arose. The strategic issues raised by the failure of Germany’s blitz against England, the RAF’s shift to nighttime attacks against Germany, and the relationship between ground invasion and strategic bombing—these were beyond his province as middle-level operational commander, and he was too busy anyway to worry about them. “The only thing I was thinking about was living for the next twenty-four hours and... trying to keep my outfit alive and the airplanes flying. ... We weren’t thinking about strategy at the time. ... We had to have an air force before we could do anything.”> In war, commanders in the field and planners in Washington both suffocated under the sheer volume of operational detail, until strategic questions often became merely further matters to be slotted to the appropriate compartments of bureaucracy. LeMay’s job was to fashion the machinery of war, not to worry about its purposes.  To a surprising degree, Arnold shared that mentality despite his much loftier perch.
