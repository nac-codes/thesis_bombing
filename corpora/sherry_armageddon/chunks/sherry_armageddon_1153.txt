The air force’s rotation policy, by limiting risks and establishing rewards validated in the civilian marketplace, looked forward to a practice commonplace in the American armed forces by the 1960s.28  To be sure, this evolution was only beginning in World War II. If risks were peculiarly finite for the airman they were nonetheless great; if most airmen calculated tangible rewards flowing from their war contract, others still saw themselves as warriors. Furthermore, wartime promises of GI benefits to veterans in some ways extended the entrepreneurial model to other servicemen.
